Plastic surgeons are seeing more patients walk through their doors asking to look like their selfies. Is this actually a growing mental health problem?

  • While seeing your own imperfections in the mirror is nothing new, having the knowledge about what you could look like without them is.
  • “The more time people spend on social media, the more their brains are being affected by the content they are exposing themselves to.
  • There’s a desire to repetitively view your social media accounts throughout the day, as they update constantly.
  • In Everyday Health’s stress survey, 57 percent of respondents, including baby boomers and those among Generation Z, reported looking at their social media accounts daily.
  • Dwelling on those perceived imperfections or flaws can make anyone feel terrible.
  • This can lead to a cyclical pattern of negative thoughts.
  • You’re stressed about a flaw, feel as if you need to change your appearance, then spend time and resources doing so, which can create more stress.
  • The difference today is the fixation on the small things that people may not have bothered to become consumed with before.
